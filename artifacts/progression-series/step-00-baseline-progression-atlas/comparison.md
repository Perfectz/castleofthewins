# Comparison

The raw before-side screenshots are necessary evidence, but they are slow to interpret in sequence. The after-side artifacts add structure, annotation, and cross-screen grouping so a reviewer can understand the shipped progression stack as one system rather than as isolated PNGs.

What the after-side artifacts add:

- `screenshots/after/mobile-progression-atlas-board.png` turns the most important phone-sized progression screens into one annotated contact sheet.
- `screenshots/after/desktop-progression-atlas-board.png` does the same for desktop so responsive differences are visible immediately.
- `screenshots/after/progression-system-map-board.png` converts the baseline into a relationship diagram that distinguishes run start, in-run growth, level gates, and persistent carry-forward systems.
- `screenshots/after/build-path-board.png` summarizes how Fighter, Rogue, and Wizard progression currently diverge without pretending that a redesign has already been chosen.

Compared with the raw baseline captures, the after-side artifacts reduce three forms of friction:

- Discovery friction: reviewers no longer need to remember filenames or open each progression state one by one.
- Interpretation friction: the boards call out where the system is creation-driven, level-driven, loot-driven, or persistence-driven.
- Publishing friction: the boards can be dropped into planning docs, design reviews, and posts without additional layout work.

What the after-side artifacts do not do:

- They do not redesign the progression systems.
- They do not normalize the live UI into an idealized mockup.
- They do not hide which screens were lightly staged for documentation instead of reached through a long organic run.

That distinction is important. This step is a baseline atlas, not a proposal deck.
