# Teaching Notes

This prompt is strong because it refuses the common trap of separating analysis from action too early. Analysis alone can produce a persuasive brief, but it does not prove that the team has identified the right leverage point. A small visible shipment tests that claim immediately.

Why this pairing matters:

- Discovery creates the decision frame. It defines the jobs, player types, tradeoffs, and success criteria.
- A tiny shipment turns those principles into something falsifiable. If the UI looks clearer in the live game, the principles are probably pointing in the right direction.
- The cost stays low. Because the changes are only naming and microcopy, the team learns something meaningful without committing to a risky redesign.

In practice, this approach produces better prompts for later steps:

- The brief explains what the menu system must do.
- The shipment shows which principles already improved the product.
- The screenshots create before and after evidence that can guide the next structural change.

That is a better foundation than either extreme: redesigning blindly or documenting endlessly without shipping anything.
